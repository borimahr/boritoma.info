Budapestről vonattal
By train from Budapest

A vonatok menetrendjei elérhetők a MÁV honlapján, online itt lehet jegyet venni. Miután megérkezel Székesfehérvárra, a vasútállomáson tudsz taxit fogni, vagy a 44-es busszal utazni az Autóbuszállomásig, ahonnan a hotel kb. 10 percnyi sétára található.

***

Use the website of the Hungarian State Railways to get information on timetables. You can buy your ticket online (make sure you have a printed copy) or in person at the railway station in Budapest. Once you are in Székesfehérvár, you may take a taxi (there is a taxi station opposite the railway station, behind the parking lot) or take Bus 44 to the Main Bus Station (Autóbuszállomás), then walk to the hotel (about 10 minutes).

City Taxi: +36.22.555.555

Fehérvár Taxi: +36.22.222.222 +36.22.343.343

(Uber and Lyft are not available in Hungary.)

***

Budapestről busszal
By bus from Budapest

Folyamatosan indulnak buszok a Népligetből Székesfehérvárra, jegyet elővételben a Népliget jegypénztárában, vagy indulás előtt a buszon lehet váltani. Miután megérkezel Székesfehérvárra, a hotel az Autóbuszállomásról kb. 10 percnyi sétára található.

***

This may be the more practical option for public transportation. Buses leave frequently from Népliget, one of the bigger bus stations in Budapest. Once you are in Székesfehérvár, walk from the Main Bus Station to the hotel (about 10 minutes). Tickets can be bought at the bus station in advance, or on the bus from the driver.

Hungarian Wedding Customs 101

Short version:

Don’t freak out if the bride gets kidnapped.
Prepare your stomach for the midnight dinner.
Bring comfortable shoes for dancing and
don’t plan on leaving before 2 (or 3 or 4) am.

Long version:

You won’t see all of these in our wedding, but you might find them interesting anyway.

Menyasszonytánc (~”bridal dance”): Also known as the “money dance”, the bridal dance involves all guests and the bride. Traditionally, the groom, father, best man or a master of ceremony would announce around midnight that the “bride is for sale” (“Eladó a menyasszony!“) while wedding music plays in the background. He would hold a large pot to collect money from all the guests to “pay” for the privilege of dancing with the bride (usually just a couple of seconds of spinning her around). Originally, this was a way of providing the couple money for their new life together. Nowadays the amount of money thrown into the pot ranges from symbolic to substantial, the emphasis is really on the gesture of contributing to the couple’s new life.

Menyecskeruha (~ “the young wife’s dress”): After the bridal dance, the couple leaves for a while and the bride changes into a red dress known as menyecskeruha. The not so subtle symbolism here refers to the bride’s transformation from being a girl to being a woman, from a being a bride to being a wife. From this point on, the bride is referred to as újasszony (“new woman”) and the groom as újember (“new man”).

Menyasszonyrablás (~”kidnapping the bride”) is an old Hungarian tradition. In the past it was taken very seriously: the groom had to watch out to ensure that the guests can’t “steal” the bride during the wedding reception; otherwise it was understood that the groom doesn’t deserve the bride. Today it it is more of a game: to prove that the groom is worthy of his wife, he has to perform a couple of tasks imposed on him by the guests. Typically, one of these includes drinking from the wife’s shoes (no worries, there is a glass involved), but the guests tend to be really creative when it comes to inventing tasks.

Porcelántörés (~”breaking the china”): the bride and / or the groom break a plate while making as much noise as possible – a tradition that originates in the old superstition that the loud noise will scare evil spirits away and thus protect the new couple.

Grillázstorta (~”walnut and sugar cake”) is an elaborate structure made of walnuts and molten sugar that the bride and the groom first break into as many pieces as possible (the number of pieces symbolizing the number of happy years together) and then both eat a piece (to symbolize the sweetness of years to come). Finally, they serve the pieces to the guests (to share the happiness with them).

Grillazstorta

Farönk fűrészelés (~”sawing the log”) is an old German tradition that made its way to Hungary as well – the bride and the groom have to cut a log symbolizing future obstacles to show that they are able to overcome those together.

Éjféli vacsora (~”midnight dinner”): Hungarian weddings are an all night affair with lots of dancing and music. To give you that extra energy to keep going into the night, there is traditionally a midnight dinner served in the wee hours, usually stuffed cabbage.
